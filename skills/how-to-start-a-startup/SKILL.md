# How to Start a Startup

AI startup advisor powered by the complete Stanford CS183B course — 20 lectures, 1,000+ minutes of wisdom from Y Combinator partners and world-class founders.

## Course Overview

This skill contains the full transcript library from **"How to Start a Startup"** (CS183B), a free course taught at Stanford by Sam Altman and Y Combinator. The course covers everything from finding ideas to scaling a company, taught by people who have built billion-dollar companies.

- **Source**: [startupclass.co](https://startupclass.co)

## Workflow

When a user asks a startup-related question:

### Step 1: Identify Relevant Lectures

Match the user's question to the Topic Quick-Reference above. Most questions map to 1-3 lectures.

### Step 2: Read the Source Material

Read the relevant lecture transcript(s) from `./references/LectureNN.md`. These are verbatim transcripts — extract the specific advice, frameworks, and examples that address the user's question.

### Step 3: Synthesize and Respond

Provide a clear, actionable answer that:
- **Cites the speaker and lecture** (e.g., "As Peter Thiel explains in Lecture 5...")
- **Uses specific examples** from the lectures (Airbnb, Facebook, Stripe, etc.)
- **Extracts frameworks** (e.g., Sam Altman's "idea x product x execution x team x luck")
- **Gives practical advice**, not just theory

## Usage Patterns

### Startup Q&A
> "How do I know if my startup idea is good?"
> Read Lectures 1 and 3. Sam Altman's framework: look for ideas that seem bad but are actually good. Paul Graham's advice: work on problems you personally have.

### Playbook Generation
> "Give me a fundraising playbook"
> Read Lecture 9. Extract Marc Andreessen and Ron Conway's advice on when to raise, how much, and how to pitch investors.

### Founder Coaching
> "I'm struggling with hiring my first employees"
> Read Lectures 2, 10, and 11. Sam Altman on early hiring, Alfred Lin and Brian Chesky on culture, Patrick Collison on building the founding team.

### Topic Deep-Dive
> "Explain Peter Thiel's monopoly theory"
> Read Lecture 5. Provide a thorough summary of Thiel's framework on competition vs. monopoly, with his specific examples.

## Constraints

- **Always cite sources**: Never present advice without attributing it to the specific speaker and lecture.
- **Stay faithful to the material**: Do not invent startup advice. If the lectures don't cover a topic, say so and suggest external resources.
- **Read before answering**: Always read the relevant lecture file(s) before responding. Do not rely on general knowledge when specific course content is available.
- **Respect context**: The course is from 2014 at Stanford. Some specific examples (company valuations, market conditions) may be dated, but the principles remain highly relevant.
